Before You Hire a Lawyer

Think about this before you spend money on legal fees: exhaust the free resources available to you first.

  • SBDC (Small Business Development Center): Free one-on-one business counseling, including referrals to attorneys. The UCF SBDC is located on campus and serves the entire Central Florida region.
  • UCF BIP network: Ask your program advisors for attorney referrals — they work with lawyers who understand international founders.
  • Florida Bar Lawyer Referral Service: Connects you with attorneys by specialty for an initial consultation (typically $25–$50 for 30 minutes).
  • Legal Aid societies: If your budget is extremely tight, some legal aid organizations provide free or reduced-cost services to entrepreneurs.

When you do hire a lawyer, ask for flat-fee quotes instead of hourly billing. Verify they have global client experience. And always get the fee agreement in writing before work begins. Central Florida's technology and aerospace sectors have created a deep pool of attorneys experienced in international business, IP, and immigration, so finding the right specialist is more achievable here than in many other U.S. markets.

Trademarks can be filed solo, but patents require a patent attorney. Budget $5K–$15K for patent work.

If you receive a legal threat, demand letter, or lawsuit, engage an attorney immediately. Don't respond on your own.

Finding the Right Attorney, They Are Not All the Same

"Lawyer" is as broad as "doctor." You would not see a cardiologist for a broken arm, and the same principle applies to legal counsel. Different situations require different specialists.

Business attorney: Entity formation, contracts, commercial disputes. Your primary legal relationship.

Immigration attorney: Visas, work authorization, immigration-linked business decisions. Essential if your business structure affects your ability to stay in the U.S. (common for E-2 or L-1 holders).

IP attorney: Trademarks, patents, copyright enforcement. A general business attorney is not enough for patent filings or trademark conflicts.

Tax attorney: International tax treaties, IRS audits, multi-country income. Use a CPA for routine work; a tax attorney for disputes involving $50,000+ liability.

Questions to ask before hiring:

  1. Do you work with international business owners?
  2. Are you familiar with E-2 / O-1 / L-1 visa implications?
  3. Do you offer flat-fee billing?
  4. Can you provide references from international founders?

A Mexican founder interviewed three attorneys — the first two had no experience with foreign-owned businesses; the third had helped 15 international founders and offered $1,200 flat-fee for entity formation and operating agreement.

Key Takeaway

The takeaway is clear. Use free resources first: SBDC counseling, UCF BIP referrals, and Florida Bar referral services. Handle simple filings yourself (SunBiz, EIN, sales tax registration). Save your attorney budget for the decisions that carry real risk — investor agreements, IP protection, immigration-linked structuring, and contracts worth serious money. Apply this principle: ask for flat fees, verify international experience, and always get the fee agreement in writing. Evaluate each legal need against the cost tables above before you commit. Make sure the right specialists are in the room before you make decisions that affect your business long-term.
