Meetings — Time Is the Currency

The beach may look relaxed, but the currents keep moving. The same is true for U.S. business meetings: the tone feels casual, but the clock is always running. What happens when you show up three minutes late to a U.S. business meeting? You lose the deal. A Peruvian founder joined a 30-minute introductory call three minutes late. The American was wrapping up by minute 25. The founder never got a second call.

Before the Meeting

  • Send an agenda. A three-bullet email shows you respect their time.
  • Confirm the day before. "Looking forward to our call tomorrow at 2 PM ET" signals professionalism.
  • Be ready five minutes early. Test your video link or arrive early enough to be composed.

During the Meeting

  • Start on time. One to two minutes of small talk is normal; five minutes feels wasteful.
  • Stay on topic. Americans value agenda-driven meetings. Tangents are politely tolerated but internally noted.
  • End with action items. Clarify who does what, by when.

The 30-Minute Default

The short version: start with 30 minutes. Asking for an hour signals you don't value their time. If productive, they'll extend or schedule a follow-up.

The Recap Email You're Not Sending

Here's where many founders lose momentum. After every meeting, send a short email within 24 hours. Include: what was discussed, what was agreed, next steps, and deadlines. This isn't optional. In U.S. business culture, if it's not in writing, it didn't happen. The recap email also protects you. It creates a paper trail. If someone later claims they didn't agree to something, you have the email to reference.

Keep it brief: 5-8 lines. Example: "Great speaking today. To recap: you'll send the vendor application by Wednesday, and I'll have our pricing sheet to you by Friday. Let me know if I missed anything."

Culture Shift — The Handshake Deal Is Dead

Think about this cultural shift. In many countries, business runs on trust, personal ties, and verbal deals. In the U.S., the contract IS the relationship. When an American business partner says "let's put it in writing," they are not expressing distrust — they are showing professionalism. Embrace this. A clear contract protects both sides and prevents the kind of misunderstandings that destroy partnerships.

Negotiation — More Direct Than You Expect

A Mexican SaaS founder spent three meetings building rapport before discussing terms. After the third, his American contact asked: "So, what's the number?" He wasn't ready with pricing. The deal stalled.

Like a riptide that pulls you out before you realize it, U.S. negotiation moves fast:

  1. Both sides state their position early. Vague responses like "we're flexible" signal you haven't done your homework.
  2. Counter-offers are limited. Expect one to two rounds. Prolonged haggling signals distrust.
  3. First offers are close to final. If someone offers $45,000, they might settle at $42,000 — but expecting $30,000 will end the conversation.
  4. Written terms matter more than handshakes. Nothing is real until it's in a signed contract or detailed email.

Networking: Quality Over Quantity

You have 60 seconds to explain who you are and what you do. Networking events are the shoreline where everyone mingles — Americans speak with you for 5-10 minutes and move on. The goal is initial connections, not closing deals.

Three networking rules founders miss:

  • Follow up within 48 hours. Reference something specific: "Great talking about supply chain logistics" beats "Nice to meet you."
  • Ask before you pitch. Read the tide of the conversation — ask about their business first. If there's a fit, briefly describe what you do.
  • Give before you ask. Share an article, make an introduction, offer an insight. The best connections form like sandbars: gradually, through steady deposits over time.

The Follow-Through Test

Consider this: Americans test new business partners with small commitments: "Send me that by Friday." "Can you get me three references?" "Share your company deck before our next call." Each of these is a quiet test. Deliver on time and you move forward. Miss the deadline, and the relationship stalls without anyone telling you why. Every small commitment you keep builds trust for the bigger ones.

The Meeting After the Meeting — Follow-Up Playbook

Send a thank-you email within 24 hours. This is expected in U.S. business culture.

Include:

  • Reference something specific from the conversation
  • Attach any materials you promised
  • Suggest a concrete next step with dates

Example: 'Great meeting today — I especially appreciated your insight on the Orlando market. As discussed, I've attached our pricing sheet. Would Thursday at 2 PM work for a follow-up call?'

If no response to your first email, send a brief follow-up.

Keep it short: 2–3 sentences maximum.

Add value: Share a relevant article, case study, or industry insight.

Don't say: 'Just checking in' or 'Following up.' These are empty phrases.

Do say: 'I came across this report on [topic we discussed] and thought of our conversation.'

Connect on LinkedIn within 48 hours of meeting.

Personalize the request: Reference where you met.

Engage with their content — comment on a post before messaging again.

Do NOT: Send a sales pitch in your connection request. Build the relationship first.

Waiting too long: After 72 hours, the meeting fades from memory.

Being too pushy: Following up daily is aggressive. Weekly is appropriate.

Forgetting names: Americans notice when you remember details from the conversation.

Not following through: If you said you'd send something, send it. Broken promises kill deals faster than anything.

Question 1 of 3

You have a first meeting with a potential U.S. business partner. What meeting duration should you request?

30 minutes is the default for U.S. business meetings, especially first meetings. Requesting more time from someone you've never met signals that you don't value their schedule. If the conversation goes well, they'll suggest extending or scheduling a follow-up.

After a productive meeting, when should you send a recap email?

The standard expectation in U.S. business is a follow-up recap within 24 hours. This email should summarize what was discussed, what was agreed, and next steps with deadlines. Waiting longer risks losing momentum and signals that you're not organized.

A founder from Ecuador is negotiating a $60,000 annual software contract with a U.S. company. The American buyer opens with an offer of $52,000. The founder, accustomed to extended negotiation in his home market, counters at $75,000 expecting to meet in the middle after several rounds. What is the most likely result in U.S. negotiation culture?

U.S. negotiation norms involve first offers close to the actual target and only one to two rounds of counter-offers. An opening of $52,000 likely means they could go to $55,000-$58,000, but a counter of $75,000 signals either a misunderstanding of U.S. norms or an unrealistic valuation. Prolonged haggling is perceived as a sign of distrust or desperation in American business culture.

Key Takeaway

The takeaway is clear. Be on time, stay focused on the agenda, and follow up in writing within 24 hours. U.S. negotiation moves fast — come prepared with specific numbers and expect one to two rounds of counter-offers. At networking events, listen first, pitch second, and always follow up the next day. Every small commitment you keep builds trust for the bigger ones. Learn to read the currents beneath the surface, and you will navigate U.S. business relationships with confidence.
