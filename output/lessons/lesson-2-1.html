Florida Has No Income Tax — But That Does Not Mean No Taxes

Here is one of the biggest reasons international founders choose Florida: there is no state personal income tax. That is real money staying in your pocket. Florida is one of only nine U.S. states with no personal income tax, and the Orlando metro area consistently ranks among the top regions in the Southeast for small business growth.

But no state income tax does not mean no taxes. Federal taxes still apply to every U.S. business, and the IRS will want to hear from you regardless of where you incorporated or how much revenue you earned.

Important: Tax obligations vary based on your entity type, revenue, and personal tax residency status. The information here is educational. Consult a qualified CPA or tax attorney for your specific situation.

ITIN, SSN, and Why Your Tax ID Matters

Think of your tax ID as the key to every building in the Financial District. Before you can file anything with the IRS, you need a tax identification number. Which one depends on your immigration status.

The bottom line? An SSN (Social Security Number) is issued to people authorized to work in the U.S. If you have a valid work visa (E-2, O-1, L-1, or H-1B), you can apply for an SSN. This is the preferred number because it connects to the broader credit and financial system.

An ITIN (Individual Taxpayer Identification Number) is for non-residents who have U.S. tax obligations but don't qualify for an SSN. You apply with IRS Form W-7, and the process takes 7 to 11 weeks.

The practical problem: some banks won't open accounts without an SSN, and if you apply for an ITIN by mail, you may need to send your original passport to the IRS. A Colombian founder was without his passport for nine weeks while the IRS processed his application.

The workaround: Apply in person at an IRS Taxpayer Assistance Center. They verify your passport on the spot and you keep it. The nearest center to Orlando is in Tampa — about two hours round trip, but you avoid mailing your passport.

How Entity Type Affects Your Tax Treatment

Now that you know the lay of the Financial District, step inside the tax office. Your entity type decides how the IRS treats your income, what forms you file, and how much you owe. That's one of the main reasons picking the right entity matters (covered in Module 1).

Let's put this in perspective. LLC (taxed as sole proprietorship or partnership): Income "passes through" to your personal tax return. You pay federal income tax at your personal rate, plus self-employment tax of 15.3% on net earnings. You file Schedule C (single-member) or Form 1065 (multi-member) with your personal Form 1040-NR.

C-Corporation: Files its own return (Form 1120) and pays corporate income tax at a flat 21% rate. If you pay yourself a salary or dividends, you also pay personal income tax on that income — "double taxation." The upside: C-Corps are the preferred structure for raising investment capital.

S-Corporation: Similar pass-through treatment as an LLC, but with potential self-employment tax savings. The catch: non-resident aliens cannot be S-Corp shareholders. No green card or citizenship means S-Corp is off the table.

A German tech founder chose a C-Corp to raise venture capital. A Mexican food service founder chose an LLC for simple pass-through taxation. The wrong choice would have cost each of them thousands in unnecessary taxes or restructuring fees.

Tax Breakdown by Jurisdiction — A Closer Look

What you owe: Corporate income tax (C-Corp: 21% flat rate) or pass-through income tax (LLC/S-Corp: reported on personal return).

Key forms: Form 1120 (C-Corp), Form 1065 (Partnership), Schedule C (Sole Proprietor).

Estimated payments: Quarterly (April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15).

International twist: ITIN required if you don't qualify for SSN. Treaty benefits may reduce withholding.

Good news: Florida has NO personal state income tax.

Corporate tax: 5.5% on taxable income over $50,000 (C-Corps only).

Sales tax: 6% state rate + county surcharge (up to 1.5% in Orange County).

Annual report: $138.75 (LLC/LP) or $150 (Corp) — due by May 1 each year through SunBiz.

Business Tax Receipt (BTR): Required by Orange County and City of Orlando — typically $25–$100/year.

Tourist Development Tax: 6% on short-term rentals (if applicable).

Impact fees: Only for construction/development businesses.

Bottom line: Local obligations are minimal for most service and technology businesses.

Tax Treaties, Check Before You Assume

Think about this: your home country may have a tax treaty with the U.S. that lowers your tax rate, removes double taxation, or changes withholding rules. For example, many European and Latin American countries have treaties that affect how dividend income or business profits are taxed. Don't assume your home-country duties go away when you form a U.S. entity, and don't assume you'll be taxed twice without checking. A CPA who handles global clients can review your treaty status in a single meeting. This one talk can save you thousands.

ℹ

Common Mistakes International Founders Make

Notice these patterns:

  • Ignoring sales tax obligation. Many international founders assume their product or service is not subject to sales tax, or they don't realize they need to register before collecting it. Florida's sales tax applies to most tangible goods and some services. (More on this in the next lesson.)
  • Waiting too long to hire an accountant who knows global clients. A general CPA may not know about ITIN filing, tax treaty rules, or FBAR needs for foreign bank accounts. By the time founders realize they need a specialist, they've already made filing mistakes that cost money to fix.
  • Missing quarterly estimated payments. If you expect to owe $1,000 or more in federal tax, you must make quarterly estimated payments — due April 15, June 15, September 15, and January 15. Miss them and you will owe penalties and interest even if you pay everything when you file your annual return. Set calendar reminders for all four dates.

  • CPA for annual tax prep (international clients): $500–$2,500/year depending on complexity
  • Quarterly estimated tax payments: Varies by income — plan for 25–30% of net profit set aside each quarter

What Would You Do?

Question 1 of 3

A single-member LLC in Florida earns $80,000 in net profit. Where does the owner report this income for tax purposes?

LLC income passes through to the owner's personal tax return. The LLC itself does not file a separate corporate return (that's a C-Corp). Florida's lack of state income tax does not eliminate federal filing requirements.

What is the main reason an international founder without a work visa would need an ITIN?

An ITIN (Individual Taxpayer Identification Number) allows non-residents to meet their U.S. tax obligations when they don't qualify for a Social Security Number. It is specifically a tax filing tool issued by the IRS. Registering with the Florida Department of Revenue is a separate sales tax step that does not require an ITIN. SunBiz registration uses an EIN, not an ITIN. A Florida driver's license is unrelated to tax filing obligations.

A founder from Brazil operates a single-member LLC in Florida and expects to owe $8,000 in federal taxes this year. She plans to pay everything when she files her annual return in April. What problem will she face?

The U.S. tax system requires quarterly estimated payments (April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15) if you expect to owe $1,000 or more. Missing these deadlines triggers penalties and interest even if you pay the full amount with your annual return. Annual payment is not the standard method when you owe over $1,000. Florida has no state income tax, so it cannot charge one as a penalty. The IRS does not convert entity types for non-compliance. The lesson describes a founder who owed $1,200 in penalties for this exact mistake.

Key Takeaway

The takeaway is clear. Florida's lack of state income tax is a real advantage, but federal obligations, quarterly estimated payments, and self-employment tax still apply to founders at every level. Budget for a CPA who works with international clients in your first year. The cost of professional help ($500–$2,500) is a fraction of the cost of penalties for filing mistakes. The Financial District has its own rules — learn them before you walk in, and they work in your favor.
