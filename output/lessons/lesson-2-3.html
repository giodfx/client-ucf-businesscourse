Banking Deep Dive — The Reality for International Founders

Why Banks Say No

You have walked past the tax offices and the accounting firms. Now you are standing in front of the tallest building at the end of the Financial District: the bank. This is the stop that trips up more international founders than any other.

You can form your LLC in two days. But getting a bank account? That can take two months and three rejections. Banks reject international founders for specific, predictable reasons:

  • No U.S. credit history. Home-country credit does not transfer. A German founder with a 15-year Deutsche Bank relationship was turned away by Chase because he had no U.S. credit score.
  • Missing documents. Every bank has a different checklist, and most do not post it clearly. Walk in missing one document and you will come back another day.
  • Entity type mismatch. Some banks prefer C-Corps over single-member LLCs. Others will not open accounts for entities less than 30 days old.
  • High-risk country flags. Anti-money-laundering rules mean founders from certain countries face added review or refusal.

What You Can Do About It

A Kenyan fintech founder applied at three banks, each asking for more KYC documents: proof of where her funds came from, notarized business records, a letter from her Kenyan bank. She eventually succeeded at a regional Orlando bank experienced with African founders, but the process took seven weeks.

International-friendly banks (early 2026): Mercury, Relay, Centennial Bank, and Valley National Bank have been more willing to work with international founders. Chase and Bank of America have stricter non-resident requirements. Always call ahead and confirm before visiting a branch.

Important: Banking policies are not uniform across institutions, and they change frequently. What worked for another founder six months ago may not apply to your situation today. Call ahead, ask specific questions, and get answers in writing when possible.

Reality Check: This Takes Longer Than You Think

Here's the thing. First bank account: 2 to 4 weeks if everything goes right. Some founders report 6 to 8 weeks with additional compliance review.

Business credit card: 3–6 months after account opening. Most require a U.S. credit score or a secured deposit.

Business credit line: 12–18 months of banking history and revenue documentation.

Plan your cash flow around these timelines. Don't expect to walk into a bank on Monday and have an account by Friday.

Building U.S. Credit History From Zero

Building U.S. credit is like establishing your reputation in the Financial District. You start as a stranger — nobody knows you. Every successful transaction, every on-time payment, adds to your standing. Your U.S. credit score starts at nothing, not low, but truly absent. Many systems reject requests when no score exists. The good news is that the Orlando metro has over 400 bank branches and credit union locations, so once you have your documents in order, options are plentiful. Here's the path to build credit:

Here is the practical takeaway. Step 1: Secured credit card. Deposit $500–$2,000 as collateral, use it for small purchases, pay in full monthly. After 6–12 months, you'll have a basic score. Capital One, Discover, and some credit unions offer secured cards to non-residents with an ITIN.

Step 2: Business credit card (not requiring personal credit). Brex and Ramp use your EIN and business revenue instead of personal credit. Mercury offers a business card connected to your Mercury account.

Step 3: Credit-builder accounts. Services like Self or Grow Credit report monthly payments to credit bureaus, building your score without a traditional credit card.

Credit affects more than banking. It shapes lease terms, vendor payment terms (Net 30/Net 60), and access to loans. A Peruvian founder paid three months' rent upfront ($6,000 in tied-up capital) because she had no U.S. credit history. Start building credit the day you open your bank account.

Payment Processors, Wire Transfers, and Moving Money

Before you leave the Financial District, stop at the service counter near the exit. Payment processors are like the district's quick-service windows — they handle transactions while you work on getting full access to the big buildings. While you work on getting a bank account, you still need to accept payments and move money.

Stripe: Requires a U.S. bank account for payouts. Fastest way to accept credit card payments online. Processing fee: 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction.

The bottom line? PayPal: Set up with an EIN and U.S. address. Useful as an interim solution, but known for freezing accounts with sudden transaction spikes. A Brazilian founder had $12,000 frozen for 21 days after a product launch triggered fraud detection.

Wise (formerly TransferWise): Gives you a U.S. account number and routing number for ACH payments. Not a full bank replacement, but excellent for moving money between countries at favorable exchange rates.

In other words, choose the right tool for the job. ACH vs. Wire vs. Check: ACH is the U.S. standard for recurring payments — free or low-cost, 1–3 business days. Wire transfers are for urgent or international payments ($25–$50 per transfer). Most B2B payments are ACH or credit card.

International wire costs add up fast. Compare your bank ($30–$50 fee + 2–3% exchange markup) against Wise ($5–$10 fee + mid-market rate) — the difference can save hundreds per month.

Watch Your Money — Wire Transfer Fees Are Real

Here's where it adds up. International wires cost $25 to $50 each direction through traditional banks, plus unfavorable exchange rates that add another 1 to 3%. ACH transfers are free or low-cost but domestic only. Plan your money movement strategy: batch international transfers to reduce fees, use Wise or similar services for better rates, and switch to ACH for domestic payments as soon as your U.S. bank account is active.

Banking Terminology — Key Terms for International Founders

ACH Transfer

Automated Clearing House — electronic bank-to-bank transfer. Takes 1–3 business days. Low or no fees.

Wire Transfer

Same-day bank transfer for large amounts. Costs $15–$30 domestic, $35–$50 international.

ITIN

Individual Taxpayer Identification Number — IRS-issued for non-residents who can't get an SSN.

KYC

Know Your Customer — bank verification process requiring ID, proof of business, and source of funds.

FDIC Insured

Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation — protects deposits up to $250,000 per account.

Chargeback

Customer disputes a card transaction. Merchant must prove the charge was legitimate or refund it.

A Quick Word About Funding and Investors

Standing at the edge of the Financial District, you can see the investor tower in the distance. Most early-stage founders are not ready to walk in there yet. Here is a question every founder asks: can you raise money from U.S. investors or get a loan? Yes, but not as easily as you might hope. Most early-stage investors want to see traction : paying customers, revenue growth, or strong user metrics — before writing a check. An idea and a pitch deck, no matter how good, rarely gets funded without evidence of U.S. market demand.

The bottom line? That's why most international founders bootstrap their first 6–12 months using personal savings, home-country revenue, or small investments from friends and family. Build traction first, then approach investors.

Why does this matter? The Small Business Administration (SBA) offers popular loan programs, but most require the borrower to be a U.S. citizen or green card holder. Non-citizen founders often don't qualify, even with a strong Florida business. Some CDFIs and microlenders have fewer restrictions, but the amounts are smaller.

We cover funding pathways in more depth in phases 2 and 3 of the UCF BIP market entry/soft landing program. Plan to fund your U.S. entry yourself for the first year.

What Would You Do?

Question 1 of 3

An international founder with no U.S. credit history wants to start building credit immediately after opening a bank account. What is the recommended first step?

A secured credit card is the standard first step for building U.S. credit from zero. You deposit cash as collateral and use the card for small purchases, paying in full each month. Applying for a business line of credit requires an existing credit history that new founders do not have. Relying on a friend's account does not build independent credit history in your name. Waiting 12 months delays credit building when you could start immediately.

A Peruvian founder just arrived in Orlando and wants to sign a commercial lease for office space. Her landlord is asking for a larger security deposit because she has no U.S. credit history. What should she have done earlier to avoid this situation?

Starting with a secured credit card the day you open your bank account builds a basic credit score within 6-12 months. SBA loans typically require U.S. citizenship or a green card, so most international founders do not qualify. Home-country credit scores do not transfer to U.S. credit bureaus. Paying cash upfront ties up capital and does not solve the underlying problem of having no U.S. credit history for future transactions.

A founder from Nigeria has registered her Florida LLC and received her EIN. She plans to visit a Chase branch in Orlando next week to open a business bank account. She has her passport and EIN letter ready. Based on what this lesson covers about documentation and physical presence, what should she do before walking into the branch?

Banks have different documentation requirements, and not every branch handles foreign-owned accounts. Calling ahead to confirm what Chase specifically needs for her situation prevents wasted trips. A passport and EIN alone are rarely sufficient — most banks also require Articles of Organization, an operating agreement, and proof of a U.S. address. Waiting for an SSN is unnecessary since many banks accept an EIN alone, but she must verify this with the specific branch. Assuming all branches work the same way ignores the lesson's emphasis that branch-level familiarity with international accounts varies widely.

Key Takeaway

Banking is one of the biggest pain points for founders. Come to every bank visit with all your documents, apply to banks that welcome foreign owners, and start building U.S. credit from day one with a secured credit card. Plan to fund yourself the first year. Investors and SBA loans have rules that take time to meet. Needs vary by bank and change often, so always check before you apply. The Financial District does not hand out trust freely — you earn it one transaction at a time.
