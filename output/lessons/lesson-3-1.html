Intellectual Property — Protecting Your Ideas in the U.S.

Welcome to the Launch Facility, Protecting What You Have Built

A founder from Chile built a successful delivery app across South America. When she expanded to Florida, she discovered that her Chilean trademark registration meant nothing in the United States. Intellectual property rights are territorial — protection in one country does not automatically extend to another. If you plan to operate in the U.S., you need U.S. protection.

There are three main types of IP protection, each covering different aspects of your business. The comparison below explains what each one protects, what it costs, and when you need it.

Trademark Registration, What Actually Happens

A trademark is the emblem that distinguishes your business from everyone else's. The registration process through the USPTO is straightforward but takes patience.

Step 1: Search the USPTO database at trademark.uspto.gov. If a conflict exists, your application is rejected and your filing fee is not refunded.

Step 2: File your application online through TEAS. Choose the right class of goods or services (45 classes). Filing fee: $250 (TEAS Plus) or $350 (TEAS Standard) per class.

Step 3: Wait for examination. A USPTO examining attorney reviews your application in 3–4 months. They may issue an "office action" requesting changes; respond within the deadline or your application is abandoned.

Step 4: Publication period. Your trademark is published in the Official Gazette for 30 days. If no one opposes, you receive your registration certificate.

Total timeline: 8–12 months. A Polish cybersecurity founder hired a U.S. trademark attorney before opening her Orlando office. The $800 she spent was a fraction of the $15,000–$25,000 she would have paid to fight a conflict later.

Reduce Risk — Register Your Trademark Before You Launch

Why does this matter? If someone else files your brand name in the U.S. first, you may lose it — even if you've used it for years in another country. The U.S. trademark system gives strong rights to the first filer. Start the process before you invest in U.S. marketing, packaging, or signage. The $250–$350 filing fee is far less than the cost of rebranding.

Common Mistakes International Founders Make

  • Assuming home-country IP protection covers the U.S. IP protection is country-by-country. Your trademark filed in Brazil, Germany, Colombia, or anywhere else gives you zero coverage in the United States. Trademarks, patents, and enforceable copyright all require separate U.S. filings. If you wait, someone else can register your brand name in the U.S. first — and they will have the legal right to use it here. File your U.S. trademark application before you start marketing in the U.S., not after.
  • Running without general liability insurance. This isn't an IP issue, but it's a Module 3 pattern: founders focus on the legal setup and forget to guard against day-to-day business risks. One lawsuit without coverage can shut you down. (More on insurance in the next lesson.)
  • Using free contract templates without legal review. Free NDA templates and service agreements from legal websites are a starting point, not a finished product. They may not follow Florida law, may be missing key clauses, or may not fit your situation. A 30-minute attorney review costs $150-$300 and can prevent costly disputes later.

Watch Your Money: What This Actually Costs

In other words, plan your IP budget early. IP, insurance, and legal costs you should budget for (as of early 2026):

  • Trademark filing (USPTO): $250–$350 per class, plus $300–$500 if using an attorney
  • Patent application: $5,000–$15,000+ including attorney fees and USPTO filing costs
  • Copyright registration: $45–$65 per work
  • NDA or contract drafting: $200–$500 per document
  • General liability insurance: $400–$800/year for small businesses
  • Professional liability (E&O): $500–$2,500/year depending on industry
  • Initial legal consultation: $0–$300 (many attorneys offer free first consultations)
  • Contract review by attorney: $200–$500 per document

What Would You Do?

Question 1 of 3

A founder has been using a brand name in Colombia for three years and now wants to sell products under that name in the U.S. What should she do first?

IP protection is territorial. A Colombian trademark has no legal effect in the U.S. The founder should search the USPTO database for conflicts and file a U.S. trademark application before investing in U.S. marketing. Waiting increases the risk that someone else files the same name first.

Which type of IP protection is most commonly needed first by international founders entering the U.S. market?

Most international founders need trademark protection first because it protects the brand name and logo they'll use in the U.S. market. Copyright exists automatically (though registration helps enforcement), and patents are only needed for genuine inventions. Trademark is the highest-priority IP filing for most new market entrants.

A founder from Ghana has developed a unique mobile payment algorithm and wants to protect both the technology and the brand name before entering the U.S. market. She has $10,000 budgeted for IP filings. Based on typical costs and timelines, which approach best uses her budget?

With $10,000, filing both a trademark ($250–$850 with attorney) and copyright ($45–$65) fits the budget while protecting the brand name and code. A patent application ($5,000–$15,000+) would consume most or all of the budget and takes 2–3 years to process. Filing the trademark and copyright now — then budgeting separately for a patent — gives her immediate protection where it matters most. Patents do not cover brand names, so option C is incorrect. Relying only on automatic copyright (option B) leaves the brand name unprotected. Filing both a patent and trademark simultaneously (option A) would exceed the $10,000 budget.

Key Takeaway

Apply this lesson before you invest in U.S. marketing: trademark your brand name first. Evaluate which IP protections your business needs, then create a filing plan. Start with a USPTO trademark search, file early, and budget $250–$850 for the trademark process. For patents, hire an attorney. For copyright, register before you need to enforce it. Your intellectual property is only as safe as the protections you put around it — act before someone else does.
